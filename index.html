JPL and NASA veterans

by:Beckett Zeigler

This website will allow you too learn about JPL and NASA veterans.

One of the men from 'Most Influential' list at JPL . Don Yeomans, manages NASA's Near-Earth Program Office at JPL. He was born and raised in snowy Rochester N.Y.he then headed for warmer weather in Southern California. As a kid, his six-inch reflecting telescope was used to marvel at the Moon's craters and Saturn's rings. He began working as a contractor for the Goddard Space Flight Center (GSFC) fresh out of graduate school (University of Maryland). With his background in solar system dynamics, he joined a Goddard team that was designing missions to comets. Later, in 1976, He joined the Jet Propulsion Laboratory (JPL) in Pasadena, Calif., working in their solar system dynamics group.

Richard Cook is also one of the menfrom 'Most Influential' list at JPL.Richard Cook is Associate Director for Flight Projects and Mission Success at NASA’s Jet Propulsion Laboratory. He was appointed Deputy Director for Solar System Exploration in 2013.he was responsible for the design, development, launch, and operations of the “Curiosity” rover that successfully landed on Mars in August 2012. His Mars Program involvement started with the Mars Pathfinder mission, for which he served as Project System Engineer and Mission Manager. Richard began his JPL career in 1989 working navigation & mission design.

Glennan was the perfect choice to lead the new organization. NASA’s first administrator. Glennan emphasized long-range goals that would yield genuine scientific and technological results. As he told everyone at the time, “Our strategy must be to develop a program on our own terms which is designed to allow us to progress sensibly toward the goal of ultimate leadership in this competition.” Recognizing political realities, Glennan also positioned NASA so that it could serve as the vehicle for competing with the Soviet Union in developing a space race.

Robert R. Gilruth had been NACA engineer since graduating from college in the latter 1930s, and shortly after NASA’s establishment, he became director of the Space Task Group at Langley Research Center. In May 1961, Gilruth’s life changed forever.Gilruth and his staff were the people who made the dreams of spaceflight real. Gilruth spent 10 years directing 25 human spaceflights, from Alan Shepard’s first Mercury flight in May 1961, through the first lunar landing by Apollo 11 in July 1969, to the Apollo 15 mission in July 1971.
